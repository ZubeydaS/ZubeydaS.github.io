Project Description

Our goal is to prototype a Carleton College food rating app called CarlsEat that allows students to easily capture images and descriptions of their food, rate dishes, and share this information with fellow users. This project addresses common issues like food quality concerns, easier feedback options for dining hall issues, and alternative sources for meals by students posting recipes themselves.

With CarlsEat, students will be able to better understand the dining options available to them, assess the quality of food, and find alternatives that meet their dietary needs while strengthening community.

UX Evaluation Overview & Statistics

Process

Our team conducted focus groups to gather user insights. The research process included:

  • 2 participants per focus group.
  • 4 total interviews utilizing focus groups.
  • A total of 8 participants contributed feedback & task completion, resulting in the measured informal UX metric averages and standard deviation statistics below.

Tasks

Below are the scenarios participants were told to imagine before announcing their tasks. Each task corresponds with one metric for the interviewer to measure.

  • Task 1: "You wanted to know someone’s opinion about the current dining hall food. How would you see that and interact with their opinions?"
    • Explanation: This task aims to evaluate how users engage with food reviews from others.
  • Task 2: "You noticed a post that coincides with your food preferences. Using the figma app prototype, how would you like the post?"
    • Explanation: This task tests the user’s ability to interact with content (like saving and liking posts).
  • Task 3: "You just had a good meal at the LDC, and you want to share your experience with other students. How would you create a post?"
    • Explanation: This task evaluates users’ ability to create a review or post and success of navigating the prototype.

Informal UX Metrics

Below are the metrics that will be measured while participants complete each task

  • Metric for Task 1: Average number of clarifying questions asked by users during task execution
    • Collection Method: Tallying the occurrences (on paper or device) while the user is completing the task.
  • Metric for Task 2: Number of errors (wrong clicks or misused elements). Eessentially, unintended behavior.
    • Collection Method: Tallying the occurrences (on paper or device) while the user is completing the task.
  • Metric for Task 3: Time taken to hypothetically capture, create, and post an image of food with a prototype
    • Collection Method: Use timer on a separate device and start timing when the user begins navigation and stop once the user shortly explains their last clicked item.

Results from UX Evaluation: Feedback & Improvements

  1. Participants strongly suggested the ability to comment on posts.


    Overview: When completing the assigned tasks, participants expected to find a comment section or higher interactable social features within the prototype. A participant also added, the engagement of students commenting on subjects like, varying cultural meals that are routinely made at the dining halls, would create interesting social engagement from students who are able to rate the accuracy of the foods from their own culture.

    Improvements: The prototype can now create comment threads under posts. Through navigating the caret buttons, the video displays checking a potential thread of users from a recipe review through the feed page and into new elements for the high-fidelity stage. The comment section allows for a social user interface, and allowed for the creation of a more interactive feed page.

    There could be comments under there.”
    I would like to comment, have it become like a Twitter thread."
    ... about cultural food where it's like, did it pass? Did it pass this culture and a person [from said culture] will respond 'yeah' or 'actually it didn’t.'"
  2. Participants found difficulties with clicking certain button elements which affected intuitiveness of the prototypes deisgn .


    Overview: The partcipants found that the caret icon used to collapse and expand various posts in the protype was quite small. As well as finding some placements such as the 'like' element was hard to intuitively spot. Resulting in higher errors for the Informal UX metrics. The perception that the button was not assessable at the mid-fidelity stage because of its small size, caused question for which features were available for participants to engage with.

    Improvements: To mitigate these concerns, the caret button on the pages have been changed to be solid, have shadow drop-down effects, and have wider area to click. Or users can choose other areas close to the caret button to activate it's purpose (like the image icon). For example, the photo icon shown in the feed is seen to be used to explore the design and create a post. Additionally, the heart icon has increased in size and can now be clicked to show a liked post. Icons and elements were experiemntally moved to create better transparency.

    If the button could be expanded a little bit to include a larger area... because that just feels more intuitive.”
    I think the button was a little bit finicky, especially if you're doing it on your phone really quickly. If you're trying to click on it, you have to click in one specific place."
    It would just be more intuitive, if the heart was there."
    I was feeling confused... this is just a prototype, so I wasn't sure if it would click on it actually."
  3. Participants recommended new engaging feautures for student interests.


    Overview: Participants brought up interesting perspectives regarding what users would potentially seek when using a food rating app. One user suggested a themed interface, similar to how the dining halls offer unique meals depending on the holiday or season. There was also the suggestion to have a rating option for the posts themselves, so not only does the meal receive a rating from the poster, but others can also rate the public post, which almost acts as a precautionary moderating system. Lastly, a participant noted that students on the platform would likely want to communicate with fellow students, especially within POC spaces.

    Improvements: This feedback opened the door to further developing the "add friends" feature or private post option. This feedback led to the creation of a text or chat option for the prototype. People would be able to create their own group chats and add friends they'd like. Additionally, as seen in the video, rating systems via upvotes and downvotes have been implemented. There is also an example of themed app changes, such as autumn colors during Halloween, as well as changed voting buttons and styling.

    Or have monthly themes like on Reddit... applying to college on Ivy day. The upvote was Harvard, and then the down vote was the Yale”
    Having a rating system... like or upvotes and downvotes, but then it can get heated."
    There's [regarding meals] like a thing where it's, 'what did POC say?'... It would be funny. Like, you know, 'does it get the black vote?'""
  4. Organize review and recipe forms.


    Overview: A participant noticed that the forms for review and the page can be organized to deliberately separate mandatory and optional items on their respective pages. The participant mentioned that users can become stuck on forms due to the confusion of what elements are needed to complete the post, which may cause user frustration.

    Improvements: This advice is great for creating a platform that is easy for users to explore. Due to this concern, and the fear that users would dismiss more important items, the order of the post elements has been changed. Basic information remains at the top, which is the same, but now, the food tags and ingredient list inputs have been prioritized over all other inputs due to the safety issues that ignoring these tags may cause. The lack of those inputs filled would diminish the purpose of CarlsEat. Thoughtful rating are encouraged and disclaimer of ingrediants are encouraged.

    sometimes we’re forced to go through forms where it makes you have mandatory ones mixing with optional."
    I think it's important to have certain stuff be mandatory… and then they can add the photo and like the optional stuff."

Future Steps from High-Fidelity Prototype & Justifications

Additional workflows : ⟶ For my focus group's 2nd metric, there was a high rate of clickable errors, which was mitigated by my team's average scores for their error metric. A future step would be to ensure that elements users would assume are usable features of the prototype are always interactable in some way unless the object is designed to be static. I consider this a future step because, although some workflows have been added to the high-fidelity prototype, there could still be more potential workflows added with additional user testing. Additionally, given the limited timeframe to make the prototype elements fully interactable within the course's timeframe, a specific future step is to add additional workflows to elements not addressed in previous user testing.

Consistent layout: ⟶ Styling the Figma prototype in a consistent format has been difficult, as this is the first time I have used Figma. A future endeavor to pursue would be to check if my button sizing, color palette, and general design choices appear consistent and intuitive in future rounds of user testing. Feedback I received highlighted that my design was well-received, but the design could still be improved in terms of consistency throughout the pages, particularly in relation to the feedback on workflows. The design layout and what users see as interactable objects directly correlate with each other. Many of the user suggestions were about buttons or not understanding which parts were interactable, so by fixing the styling, user feedback would be satisfied. A complete user interface design with cohesion would help future viewers of the prototype.

Drawbacks of Feedback: ⟶ However, with the addition of several new features, I found it challenging to maintain an aesthetically pleasing design that didn’t feel overcrowded. At times, it felt like there were too many features in one space, making the interface feel a bit cluttered. Despite following the feedback and requirements, a more minimalist approach to design might help improve the overall user experience. Simplifying the interface while retaining all necessary features could make it easier for users to navigate and engage with the app design without feeling overwhelmed.

Comment Options: ⟶ If our team hypothetically restarted the Requirements Generation, I believe it would have been best to add a requirement indicating that some sort of comment section is mandatory, since my user feedback primarily focused on not being able to comment on users' posts. Additionally, my teammates naturally added a comment section in their prototypes, so adding this requirement would not be labor-intensive, but rather would add transparency to a feature I initially overlooked.

Themed Months or Holiday Features: ⟶ Users showed interest in the concept of themed months or holiday features (e.g., special design changes for Halloween or Valentine's Day). A future step would be to implement a toggle feature that allows users to switch between different themes for different months or holidays, enhancing the fun and personalized experience of the app.

Bon App Menu: ⟶ One suggestion was the integration of the design with existing dining services, such as Carleton’s menu options. The Menu page has a menu button for each dining hall, but is not interactable due to Bon App's changing daily menus. A future step would be to explore how adding Bon App's menu could be achieved through my design. This would provide more relevant and real-time information to users.

Accessibility Features: ⟶ One area that could be improved is the accessibility of the prototype. A participant mentioned that they found the design easy to read which surprised them to due their dyslexia. Which leads to other improvements in design that could benefit different users with varying needs like if someone could use this design with features like screen reader compatibility, high-contrast modes, or keyboard navigation. A future step would be to ensure the prototype complies with WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) to improve accessibility for all users, particularly those with disabilities. This would positively impact the range of users needed to test the design.

Team UX Research Process for Requirements Elicitation Protocols

Our team conducted a combination of semi-structured interviews and focus groups to gather user insights. The research process included:

  • 2 one-on-one semi-structured interviews.
  • 3 participatory design interviews utilizing focus groups.
  • A total of 8 participants contributed feedback, resulting in 5 interviews conducted by team members overall.

This interview style allowed for direct questions and answers, leading to actionable user insights. Some notable user feedback included:

“I think it would be cool to see what other people are making, and I might get ideas from that.”
“It would be great for people to take photos of the meals. Like, 'Hey guys, this is what it looks like.' You know what I mean?"
“I have allergies, and Carleton is not so great about cross-contamination.”

Key themes that emerged from the research included concerns about allergens, convenience, pricing, meal quality, and social engagement.

Team Analysis Process for Requirements Elicitation Protocols

Our team utilized axial coding and affinity diagramming to analyze our interview transcripts and build consensus for our requirement statements. The process involved six key steps:

  1. Initial Axial Coding
  2. Affinity Diagramming
  3. Creating a Codebook
  4. Axial Coding with the Codebook
  5. Consensus Building
  6. Requirements Generation

Our affinity diagram categorizes the axial coding outcomes from the transcripts. We identified phenomena, conditions, actions, and consequences using blue sticky notes. These were connected to yellow sticky notes, which summarized team-generated short descriptors. Each yellow sticky note mapped to a section, color-coded based on the type of concept (phenomena, conditions, actions, or consequences), corresponding to axial codes from each team member.

This process helped us develop a comprehensive codebook that refined our understanding of the data until we reached a consensus on our interviews. Ultimately, we generated nine requirement statements.

How the Prototype Addresses User Needs for Figma Prototype 1

Based on our user research and the elicitation process, which included focus groups and interviews with Carleton students, this prototype offers an intuitive platform for sharing dining experiences. Students will be able to rate food, capture images, and identify alternatives for specific dietary needs, particularly for those with food allergies or dietary restrictions. This addresses the need for an easy to navigate app that combines multiple functions of the dining hall experience into one app, while simultaneously allowing users to provide their input and feedback to create a better dining hall experience.

Making the app accessible to non-Carleton users was a topic of discussion during one focus group interview. This was a thoughtful suggestion to use in the planning so that parents or visitors can see dining hall options and reviews in an efficient manner. Another area of concern was the general quality of the dining hall food and issue of cross contamination with allergens. This concern can be properly addressed with CarlsEat due to the social element of the app. Users can leave reviews of foods they enjoyed, but also of those that might pose risk. This social element could cause change if enough people leave reviews of the same issue, rather than the feedback option provided currently which creates a more confrontational environment. With this interface, feedback remains constructive and efficient.
